Whole-body Reinforcement Learning on a Wheeled Quadruped

This project aims to improve agile and stable locomotion on wheeled quadrupeds operating in unstructured outdoor environments. The objective is to develop a whole-body reinforcement learning (RL) policy capable of coordinating wheel actuation and leg dynamics to maintain stability, reduce slip, and adapt to varying terrain conditions.

The work evaluates a model-free PPO policy trained in Isaac Sim and MuJoCo, with a focus on understanding sim-to-real transfer issues such as friction mismatch, wheel–ground interaction, and dynamic stability at higher speeds. I created the simulation environment, tuned reward structures, and benchmarked baseline policies across diverse terrain types.

Initial results show strong performance in simulation but highlight degradation in real-world rollout due to unmodeled friction losses and terrain irregularities. My contribution includes simulation design, RL policy evaluation, and systematic analysis of failure modes to guide the next phase of whole-body policy development.

LiDAR-based Dynamic Object Detection with a Hybrid Geometric-Learning Approach

This research addresses the challenge of detecting dynamic obstacles in cluttered environments to improve robot safety and perception robustness. The project objective is to design a fast and reliable dynamic object detection pipeline suitable for real-time operation on Boston Dynamics Spot.

The method combines geometric free-space clustering with a lightweight learning-based classifier to identify dynamic object candidates from LiDAR scans in under 100 ms. I implemented and evaluated this hybrid pipeline, benchmarking performance across indoor/outdoor datasets.

The system successfully detects motion-induced changes while maintaining high precision and low latency. My role includes algorithm development, model evaluation, and real-robot deployment.

Autonomous Navigation and Gas Detection on a Wheeled Robot

This project advances autonomous gas-monitoring systems to support safe inspection of industrial environments. The goal is to create a rover capable of waypoint navigation while collecting real-time gas concentration data.

I integrated the FLIR gas sensor, dual-antenna RTK GNSS, RealSense depth camera, and an Intel NUC onto an AgileX Scout Mini platform, handling hardware installation, network configuration, and ROS interfacing. I developed a rule-based obstacle-avoidance and waypoint-navigation stack, enabling robust operation in structured environments.

The final system provides reliable autonomous traversal and real-time gas-mapping capabilities. I also created a GUI to visualize GNSS position, sensor telemetry, and satellite imagery, contributing to system usability and operator awareness. My role spanned hardware integration, autonomy software, and visualization design.

Aspiration-assisted End-cut Coaxial Needle Biopsy Device

This project investigates how aspiration-assisted needle designs can improve biopsy sample yield for prostate cancer diagnostics. The objective was to evaluate a novel coaxial biopsy needle and quantify its performance relative to commercial devices.

I led an independent honors thesis, designing the experimental protocol, collecting tissue-phantom samples, and analyzing force–stiffness data using encoder and strain-gauge measurements. The prototype needle produced samples 102% heavier than two clinical devices, demonstrating significantly improved tissue acquisition. Force-stiffness correlations achieved R² = 0.96 (encoder) and R² = 0.94 (strain gauge), validating the consistency of the method.

My contribution included experimental design, data collection, analysis, and thesis writing, culminating in a successful defense before the honors committee.
